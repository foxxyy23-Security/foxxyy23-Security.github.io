Why SOPs Fail (And How to Fix Them)

I’ve seen it a dozen times. A new analyst joins the team, an alert fires, and they freeze. They open the "Standard Operating Procedure" (SOP), but instead of a checklist, they find a 40-page Word document full of compliance jargon, out-of-date screenshots, and zero actionable steps.

Most SOPs fail because they are written for auditors, not for operators. In the heat of an incident, no one wants to read a novel. They want to know: What do I do right now?

The "Pilot's Checklist" Approach

When an engine fails at 30,000 feet, pilots don't read a manual on thermodynamics. They pull out a "Quick Reference Handbook" (QRH). It’s bold, it’s brief, and it saves lives.

Cybersecurity SOPs should be the same. Here is my structure for an effective response guide:

  • Trigger: Exactly what alert name or condition starts this process?
  • Triage (The "Kill" Step): 3 steps to determine if this is a False Positive. If yes, close it. If no, proceed.
  • Containment: The exact command to isolate the host. Not "isolate the host," but sentinelctl isolate -hostname <ID>.
  • Escalation: Who do I call if this goes sideways?

Updating the Culture

The best SOP is a living document. I encourage my team to "redline" documents during an incident. If a step was wrong, mark it. If a screenshot changed, flag it. We review these notes in our post-mortem, ensuring the next person doesn't trip over the same wire.

Empower the defense by giving them tools that actually work.
